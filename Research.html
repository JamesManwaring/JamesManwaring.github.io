Research

My research mostly focuses on criminal law and philosophy. My papers broadly speaking cover (1) criminal doctrine, (2) criminal law theory, and (3) other areas of interest.

Criminal doctrine: I wrote my DPhil (PhD) thesis on the criminal law's incapacity rules, such as insanity, diminished responsibility, and infancy. More recently I've written about various aspects of criminal law, including defences, complicity, and homicide.

Criminal law theory: I've written about proportionate punishment, and plan to return to some questions about principles by which to evaluate the criminal law (eg fair labelling).

Other: I'm interested in a lot of things besides criminal law, and have a few papers to write on law reform, tort law, and moral philosophy.

Published

  1. ‘Windle Revisited’ [2018] Criminal Law Review 987 [paywalled article; final draft]

    2. In English law, the insanity defence is available if the defendant ‘did not know he was doing what was wrong’ (part of the famous M’Naghten rules). English law interprets this very restrictively to mean 'Did not know it was legally wrong. I argue that this interpretation was based on flawed reasoning.

  2. ‘Proportionality’s Lower Bound’ (2021) 15 Criminal Law and Philosophy 393-405 [open access]

    3. When is punishment proportionate? Most philosophers care more about inflicting disproportionality harsh punishment rather than disproportionality lenient punishment. I propose that punishment isn't disproportionality harsh if if it imposes costs on D no greater than the costs which D intentionally caused to others. (The titular ‘lower bound’ on disproportionate punishment.) I show how this fairly ecumenical standard implies that punishments could be significantly more harsh than current sentencing rules without being disproportionate. The reason for this is that crimes impose significantly higher costs (on society generally) than is often appreciated.

  3. ‘Homicide: Losing Control’ (2024) 83 Cambridge Law Journal 210-213 [open access]

    4. Harry Turner killed his wife Sally by stabbing her 68 times. At his murder trial, he tried to claim the partial defence of loss of control. This defence has three elements, and the trial judge said that none were present. He was duly convicted of murder. But the Court of Appeal Turner was convicted upon his retrial.)

  4. ‘Inferring Culpability from Negligence’ (2025) Singapore Journal of Legal Studies (forthcoming)

    5. Criminal liability often turns on whether the defendant was negligent. You are negligent if you failed to do what the ‘reasonable person’ would have done. This standard is controversial because you might fail to do what the reasonable person would have done through no fault of your own. (Eg if you are unable to live up to that standard.) Andrew Simester, a leading philosopher of criminal law, has defended the negligence standard, while arguing that it should be relativised to account for defendants with low intelligence. I critically assess his account, and argue that his conclusion is unstable: he doesn't offer a compelling argument to relativise the standard to low intelligence but not other shortcomings.

  5. ‘Unnecessary Self-defence’ (under review)

    6. I argue that force used in self-defence can be lawful even if it is unnecessary.
